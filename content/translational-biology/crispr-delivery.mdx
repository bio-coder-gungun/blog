CRISPR-Cas9 has been editing genomes in academic labs since 2012. The gap between *can edit* and *can treat patients* is almost entirely a delivery problem. This post unpacks the three dominant delivery modalities and where each breaks down.

## What needs to get in

A functional CRISPR edit requires two things to co-localize inside the target cell nucleus:
1. The **guide RNA (gRNA)** — a 20-nucleotide sequence complementary to the genomic target
2. The **Cas9 nuclease** — a ~4.2 kDa protein or its mRNA equivalent

The combined cargo (~4.5 kb of mRNA + 100 nt gRNA, or a ~6 MDa ribonucleoprotein) must:
- Survive the bloodstream without degradation
- Evade the immune system
- Cross the endothelial barrier (for non-liver targets)
- Enter the target cell (receptor-mediated endocytosis)
- Escape the endosome before lysosomal degradation
- Enter the nucleus

Each step is a bottleneck. The industry refers to this collectively as the **"last mile problem"**.

## Adeno-associated virus (AAV)

AAV is the gold standard for *in vivo* gene therapy. Its natural tropism for post-mitotic cells (neurons, cardiomyocytes, hepatocytes) and well-characterized serotype-tissue relationships make it predictable.

**Strengths:**
- Long-term expression from episomal DNA (doesn't integrate, low oncogenic risk)
- Multiple serotypes with defined tissue tropism (AAV9 → CNS, AAV8 → liver, AAVrh10 → muscle)
- Clinical track record (Luxturna, Zolgensma)

**Limitations:**
- **4.7 kb packaging capacity** — Cas9 from *S. pyogenes* is 4.2 kb, barely fitting with minimal promoter and gRNA expression cassette. Dual-vector split-Cas9 approaches exist but halve efficiency.
- **Pre-existing immunity** — up to 70% of humans have neutralizing antibodies to common serotypes from prior natural infections. Re-dosing is essentially impossible.
- **Manufacturing cost** — $\sim$\$500K–\$1M per patient dose at commercial scale.

<Callout type="warning">
AAV integrates at low frequency (~0.1%), but in rapidly dividing cells this can drive clonal expansion. The 2022 death in a systemic AAV-delivered gene therapy trial has prompted FDA re-evaluation of high-dose AAV safety in non-dividing and dividing tissues alike.
</Callout>

## Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)

LNPs became clinically validated via the COVID-19 mRNA vaccines. The core structure is a ~100 nm particle with an ionizable lipid that is neutral at physiological pH (minimizing toxicity) but becomes cationic at endosomal pH (promoting endosomal escape).

**The four-component formulation:**
1. **Ionizable lipid** (e.g., MC3, ALC-0315) — enables pH-dependent endosomal escape
2. **Helper lipid** (DSPC) — structural stability
3. **Cholesterol** — membrane fluidity
4. **PEGylated lipid** — prevents aggregation and immune recognition

**For CRISPR:** LNPs can carry Cas9 mRNA + gRNA. Since mRNA is transient, the editing window is days (reducing off-target effects). Intellia's ITX-4001 uses LNPs and showed ~90% ATTR knockdown in Phase 1.

**The liver trap:** LNPs are efficiently taken up by hepatocytes via ApoE-mediated endocytosis. For liver diseases (ATTR amyloidosis, hemophilia, hypercholesterolemia), this is a feature. For everything else, it is the problem.

## Extracellular vesicles and engineered exosomes

The newest entrant. Cells naturally secrete ~30–150 nm vesicles that carry RNA cargo to neighboring cells. Loading CRISPR into exosomes promises:
- Low immunogenicity (self-derived membranes)
- Natural cell tropism
- No packaging size limit

The problem: loading efficiency is low (~0.01 molecules of mRNA per vesicle), and scale-up is technically demanding. No LNP-competitive clinical data yet, but Codiak, Evox, and Carmine Therapeutics are all in early trials.

## A rough comparison

| Property | AAV | LNP | Exosome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Payload size | 4.7 kb max | No limit | No limit |
| Duration | Long-term | Transient | Transient |
| Primary tissue | CNS, liver, muscle | Liver | TBD |
| Immunogenicity | High (pre-existing Ab) | Moderate (anti-PEG) | Low |
| Manufacturing | Complex | Scalable | Hard to scale |
| Clinical stage | Approved | Phase 1–3 | Phase 1 |

## Where the field is going

The most exciting development is **tissue-targeted LNPs** — replacing the ApoE-dependent liver targeting with selective organ targeting (SORT) lipids or antibody-conjugated particles. Tian et al. (2020) showed that adding a fifth, charged lipid to the formulation shifts tropism from liver to spleen or lung in mice. Whether this translates to humans is the next frontier.
